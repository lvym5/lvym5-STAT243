Basic Data Acquisition

Basic data acquisition: includes types of graphs, variables, spreads, and IQR.
The variables are split into two categories: categorical and quantitative.

Categorical variables (also called qualitative variables) are variables that take on a certain number based on its qualitative property.
Example: On a 6 sided die, there are 6 options: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. There are 6 options available. Do not confuse this with quantitative variables. Even though the pips on a die are numbers, they represent different categories, which therefore make them categorical.

The first of the quantitative variables is called a discrete variable. In statistics, a discrete variable is categorized as an enumerable list of values or possibilities of all numbers in a list.
Example: Suppose we are counting bears in a forest. The variable bears is a discrete variable because there could be one or zero bears in the forest, but there also could be one million. We are just unsure.

The second of the quantitative variables is called a continuous variable. A continuous variable is defined as not being a finite number. This means that it is usually not a round or whole number.
Example: Suppose you are running a race; your time may be recorded to the nearest hundreth of a second, but your race time is continuous, as there is still yet to be determined the thousandth, ten thousandth, hundred thousandth, etc second of your race time.
