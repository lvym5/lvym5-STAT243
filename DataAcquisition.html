Basic Data Acquisition

Basic data acquisition: includes types of graphs, variables, spreads, and IQR.
The variables are split into two categories: categorical and quantitative.

Categorical variables (also called qualitative variables) are variables that take on a certain number based on its qualitative property.
Example: On a 6 sided die, there are 6 options: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. There are 6 options available. Do not confuse this with quantitative variables. Even though the pips on a die are numbers, they represent different categories, which therefore make them categorical.

The first of the quantitative variables is called a discrete variable. In statistics, a discrete variable is categorized as an enumerable list of values or possibilities of all numbers in a list.
Example: Suppose we are counting bears in a forest. The variable bears is a discrete variable because there could be one or zero bears in the forest, but there also could be one million. We are just unsure.

The second of the quantitative variables is called a continuous variable. A continuous variable is defined as not being a finite number. This means that it is usually not a round or whole number.
Example: Suppose you are running a race; your time may be recorded to the nearest hundreth of a second, but your race time is continuous, as there is still yet to be determined the thousandth, ten thousandth, hundred thousandth, etc second of your race time.

Types of distributions: graphs and charts
A distribution of a variable gives the value along with its frequency

We use bar charts for categorical data and histograms for quantitative data.
If a graph is not symmetric, then it is considered skewed.

  • Right skew: tail points to right (mean is larger than the median)
  • Left skew: tail points to left (median is larger than the mean)

    • Make sure to take note of the shape of a graph when observing it:
  • Number of nodes
  • Type of symmetry
  • Pattern
  • Deviation of pattern (also called outliers)

    • Other things to know:
  • Interpolation does not work for categorical data
  • Sensitivity vs. Restraint: Data is said to be sensitive if it responds drastically to outliers

    • Important spreads to be aware of

    Range tells us how far apart the edges of our data are. This is found by subtracting the minimum from the maximum.

    Interquartile Range, also known as Q3-Q1, takes the first and third quartiles of the 5 number spread. It is said to be very resistant to outliers.

    Standard Deviation is sensitive to outliers, but it is a way to measure how much a set of numbers vary from their average value. The equation is found below:
    \( \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum{(x - \bar{x})^2}}{n - 1}} \), where:

  • \( \sigma \) is the standard deviation
  • \( x \) is each individual data point
  • \( \bar{x} \) is the mean of the data
  • \( n \) is the number of data points

    • The 5 number spread and the bell shaped curve

    Our 5 number spread consists of:

  • The maximum
  • The third quartile (Q3)
  • The median
  • The first quartile (Q1)
  • The minimum

    • The 1.5 IQR rule states that points are considered outliers if the whiskers exceed 1.5x the distance of the first and third quartiles. If our data is symmetric and a bell shaped appearance, 95% of our data will lie 2 standard deviations on either end of the mean.
  • If a graph is flat, it is called uniform

    • The Z-score is generally used when our data is symmetric and bell shaped. It measures the distance between a data point and the mean using standard deviations.
    Z-score formula = $\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$

    Types of multivariate and bivariate studies

    Observation studies are studies that follow groups to compare and contrast characteristics. This does not include the observer changing anything about the study.
    Example: A researcher watches monkeys in the wild to see how they interact with each other in the environment.

    Experimental studies are studies in which researchers manipulate explanatory variables (called factors). Features of experiments include:

  • Control group
  • Blinding
  • Double blinding
  • Randomization of assigned treatments

    • Example: A researcher wants to know if a pill actually lowers blood pressure. They divide a group of randomly chosen people into two groups, one group using the actual pill and the other taking a placebo. The results are then studied.

    Displays of association
    When there are two variables, there are different displays to show the association.
    Both variables are categorical: use a 2 way table.
    Both variables are quantitative: use a scatterplot.
    One variable is categorical and the other is quantitative: use a boxplot.

    To find the correlation coefficient, use the following formula:
    \( r = \frac{\sum ((x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}))}{\sqrt{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2}} \)

    Correlation properties:

  • Requires two quantitative variables
  • Value is always between -1 and 1
  • If r = 1, then the xy relationship is a perfect line, with a positive slope
  • If r = -1, then the xy relationship is a perfect negative slope
  • r has the same sign as the line through the points
  • r remains the same independent of which variable is considered explanatory
  • r is independent of units
  • r2 is the coefficient of determination, which measures what portion of variability in response is explained by a linear relationship (always positive)

    • Residuals and Interpolation
    A residual is the difference between the actual and observed y value and the predicted one.
    Residual formula: \( \varepsilon = y_i - \hat{y}_i \)
    Interpolate: estimating between the range of data (more reliable than extrapolation)
    Extrapolate: estimating outside the range of data
