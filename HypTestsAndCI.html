Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Intervals

Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Intervals: calculating p values to make assumptions about a population
Confidence Intervals

Inference: Using sample info to draw conclusions about the population. We achieve this by using CI construction and hypothesis testing

Confidence intervals have the format: point estimate ≠ z score * SE

A z score allows us to describe the standard deviations of sample means

  • To find the z score, we use qnorm(1-(1-level of confidence/2)) in R

    • Hypothesis testing contains two types of hypotheses:

  • H0, our null hypothesis, that typically assumes that there is no relationship between the samples
  • Ha, our alternative hypothesis answers our supposed research question

    • Null distribution: sampling distribution of your sample statistic under the assumption that H0 is true.
    What is the relative frequency of getting a result like my sample or something more extreme given the null hypothesis?

    It is typical to use a significance level α of 0.05, as we want a p value lesser or equal to α.
    A p value smaller than 0.05 tells us that the probability of obtaining the test results at least as extreme as those observed during our test is less than 5%

    Inference Tests

    One Proportion Inference Tests

  • Our test statistic p is provided for our null and alternative hypotheses
  • We use an iid sample becasue the independence of draws are valid as long as the population does not exceed 10% of the actual population

    • The distribution for x and p̂ are not the same:

  • Distribution for x: \(x \sim N(np, \sqrt{np(1-p)})\)
  • Distribution for p̂: \(\hat{p} \sim N(p, \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}})\)

    • Type Errors and Limitations

    Type 1 Error: The null hypothesis is true but we reject it
    Example: We jail an innocent man

    Type 2 Error: The null hypothesis is false but we fail to reject it.
    Example: We let a guilty man go free

    Two Proportion Inference Procedures

    In two proportion inference procedures, we want samples from two populations which are independent and justify the normal approximation.

  • \(n_1 p_1 \geq 10\), \(n_1 (1 - p_1) \geq 10\)
  • \(n_2 p_2 \geq 10\), \(n_2 (1 - p_2) \geq 10\)

    • Our standard error is \(SE = \sqrt{\frac{p_1 \cdot (1 - p_1)}{n_1} + \frac{p_2 \cdot (1 - p_2)}{n_2}}\), where:

  • \(p_1\) is the sample proportion of the first group
  • \(p_2\) is the sample proportion of the second group
  • \(n_1\) is the sample size of the first group
  • \(n_2\) is the sample size of the second group
